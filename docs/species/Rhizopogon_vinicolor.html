Rhizopogon vinicolor

Date: October 13, 2025

1. Taxonomy & Nomenclature

  • Scientific Name (current): Rhizopogon vinicolor

  • Synonyms / Former Names: NA

  • Common Name(s): Wine-colored Pogie

  • Taxonomic Authority / Citation: Mycobank

  • Higher Classification (Kingdom / Phylum / Class / Order / Family):
    Fungi -> Dikarya -> Basidiomycota -> Agaricomycotina -> Agaricomycetes -> Agaricomycetidae -> Boletales -> Rhizopogonaceae -> Rhizopogon -> Rhizopogon Vinicolor

2. Morphology & Identification

  • Fruiting Body Description:

    • Size: 10 to 33 mm in diameter
    • Shape: rounded or irregularly shaped
    • Color: dusty white color and later red
    • Texture: few rhizomorphs at the base

  • Cap / Pileus Characteristics:
    Single layer, becomes wine red when exposed.

  • Gills / Pores / Spines / Teeth / Etc.:
    Filled with basidiospores.

  • Stipe / Stem / Attachment / Ring / Volva / Base:
    Base with few rhizomorphs.

  • Spore Print Color:
    NA

  • Microscopic Features (if available): (spore size & shape, cystidia, etc.)

    • Trama filled with basidiospores
    • Basidiospores elliptical to ovid
    • 5-9 x 3-5 um in size
    • Do not have a septum, smooth, and hyaline
    • Mycelial cords are few in number of absent altogether

  • Other Diagnostic Characters:

    • Odor: slightly fruity
    • Bruising reactions: becomes vinaceous when exposed or bruised
    • Color change: peridium becomes wine red when exposed. The body starts as a dirty color with tinges of yellow and staining pink, then becomes wine red.
    • Texture: rubbery

  • Similar Species / Potential Confusions:

    • Rhizopogon vesiculosus, and some species of Amanita (early in their life cycles)

3. Distribution & Habitat in Washington

  • Range (counties or regions):
    Clatlam, Snohomish, Kitsap, Pierce, Thurston, Lewis, Cowlitz, Clark, Skamania, Klickitat, Kittilas, Pend Oreille

  • Geographic Distribution in WA:
    Western Coastal Washington and Central Cascades

  • Elevation Range:
    Wide elevation range: sea level to 610m (Western Washington) and up to 1520 m (Central Cascades)

  • Habitat Types / Substrate Preferences: (forest type, soil, leaf litter, dead wood, etc.)

    Old growth Douglas fir stands, soil, roots

  • Host Associations (if applicable):

    Douglas fir

4. Seasonality & Phenology

  • Months of Fruiting in WA:
    The primary fruiting period is in the spring from February to July, but they can be found year round.

  • Seasonal Notes: (peak times, moisture dependence, etc.)
    Fruiting season is triggered by rain. The best time to look is in the weeks after significant rain has saturated the forest floor. The fungus itself is drought-resistant and can help the host trees survive dry spells.

5. Ecology & Role

  • Ecological Function:
    Ectomycorrhizal relationship with Douglas fir, saprotroph.

  • Substrate / Decomposition Role:
    Saprotrophyic.

  • Interactions:

    They have a mutualistic relationship with Douglas firs. They breakdown leaf litter and rotting wood for nutrient availability for the Douglas Firs in exchange for carbohydrates.

    Western red-backed vole feeds on their fruiting bodies, the spores pass through their guts, and are deposited in fecal pellets. These spores remain viable and form associations with uninfected trees. 

  • Environmental Sensitivity / Indicators:

    If a forest is clear-cut and the dead wood is removed, Rhizopogon vinicolor stops fruiting, the vole population dies out, and newly planted trees fail to thrive. Tree growth is positively associated with the presence of R. vinicolor.

6. Conservation, Abundance & Threats

  • Abundance / Rarity in WA:
    Highly abundant in Douglas fir stands in Washington. 

  • Conservation Status (state, federal, red list):
    Not of concern

  • Threats / Pressures:
    Clear-cutting of old-growth Douglas fir stands

  • Management or Monitoring Notes:

    Preserve old-growth Douglas firs to maintain the relationship and presence between Douglas firs, voles, and R. vinicolor.

7. Use / Ethnobiology / Toxicology

  • Edibility / Toxicity:
    It is edible, but due to its small size, rubbery/foam-like texture, and mild flavor, some say it requires more effort in the kitchen than the results justify.

  • Traditional / Cultural Uses:
    No known traditional or cultural uses.

  • Medicinal / Bioactive Compounds:
    It has the ability to inhibit the growth of certain root pathogens like Phytophthora cinnamomi and Pythium debaryanum. 

  • Cautions / Warnings:
    Advised not to eat unless you are 100% sure of the identity, as some species of Amanita, which are highly toxic and begin life in an egg-shaped sac, may look similar.

10. Other Notes & Observations

  • Field Notes / Observations: Some have observed its existence around pines and other firs besides the Doug fir.
  • Questions / Uncertainties:
    • Uncertain about the elevation range 
    • No cultural importance was written about, but it could exist, just not in the literature. 
  • Fun facts

    • R. vinicolor can grow under significant water stress and remains active under drought conditions. This leads to increased establishment and survival rate of inoculated tree seedlings on harsh, dry sites. 

    • R. vinicolor can enhance the net photosynthesis of Douglas-fir seedlings. 

    • R. vinicolor is a valuable model organism for studying the population genetics of ectomycorrhizal fungi due to its clear association with Douglas-fir and its distinct genetic markers.
