Beauveria bassiana

Date: October 13, 2025

1. Taxonomy & Nomenclature

  • Scientific Name (current): Beauveria bassiana
  • Synonyms / Former Names: Botrytis bassiana
  • Common Name(s): White muscardine fungus or Icing Sugar Fungus
  • Taxonomic Authority / Citation: (Bassi 1835)
Scientific Classification
Rank Name
Kingdom Fungi
Phylum Ascomycota
Class Ascomycetes
Order Hypocreales
Family Cordycipitaceae
Genus Beauveria

2. Morphology & Identification

  • Fruiting Body Description: White, fluffy mold that grows on a host (size, shape, color, texture)
  • Cap / Pileus Characteristics: This fungi does not have a cap
  • Gills / Pores / Spines / Teeth / Etc.: This fungi does not have gills, pores, spines, or teeth
  • Stipe / Stem / Attachment / Ring / Volva / Base: This fungi does not have a fruiting body with any of these morphological features
  • Spore Print Color: White or yellowish white
  • Microscopic Features (if available): Produces white spore balls made of single-celled conidia. Has conidiogenous cells that are short, ovoid, and have a rachis; septate hyphae.
  • Other Diagnostic Characters: This fungi is powdery while sporulating.
  • Similar Species / Potential Confusions: Can be confused with Metarhizium and Akanthomyces species as well as other Beauveria species

3. Distribution & Habitat in Washington

  • Range (counties or regions): Distributed worldwide, generally seems to be more abundant on coasts and islands. Found from Everett down to Chehalis
  • Geographic Distribution in WA: Distributed mostly along the Western side of Washington
  • Elevation Range: Mainly found at low to mid elevations, seems to be found in forested areas
  • Habitat Types / Substrate Preferences: Occurs in soil, prefers humid, temperate climates
  • Host Associations (if applicable): often seen on insects (ants, beetles, moths, worms, bees), has been observed on reptiles

4. Seasonality & Phenology

  • Months of Fruiting in WA: Has been most observed from June to October
  • Seasonal Notes: Peaks in warm, moist weather during these months

5. Ecology & Role

  • Ecological Function: Saprotroph and/or parasite
  • Substrate / Decomposition Role: lives in soil, finds insect as a host, takes over insect body, and eventually decomposes it
  • Interactions: Interacts with insects
  • Environmental Sensitivity / Indicators: As the weather gets warmer due to climate change, this fungi will likely become more abundant

6. Conservation, Abundance & Threats

  • Abundance / Rarity in WA: Seems to be moderately abundant in WA
  • Conservation Status (state, federal, red list): Does not have a global conservation status, is listed as vulnerable in Alberta and Manitoba, CA
  • Threats / Pressures: Climate change, fungi does well in warm, moist environments
  • Management or Monitoring Notes: Does not seem to be managed or monitored at this time, not seen as a threat

7. Use / Ethnobiology / Toxicology

  • Edibility / Toxicity: Not edible, spores may worsen breathing difficulties, at least one case of human infection has been reported in a person with a suppressed immune system
  • Traditional / Cultural Uses: Commonly used for pest control
  • Medicinal / Bioactive Compounds: This fungus is a contact mycoinsecticide that is registered for use in the US on pests and agricultural and horticultural crops
  • Cautions / Warnings: Can be harmful to humans if ingested and can cause eye irritation. Could harm pollinators and other beneficial insects as well as fish.

10. Other Notes & Observations

  • Field Notes / Observations: Looking at iNaturalist observations most recorded findings of this fungi in Washington state have been in natural areas or forests and have been seen mainly on insects.
  • Questions / Uncertainties: Will it eventually become harmful to use this fungi as a pesticide?
  • Fun facts: This fungus is a cordiceps and can kill insects within 4-10 days. It is able to infect 700 different species of insects and can colonize plants to help them resist pests.
