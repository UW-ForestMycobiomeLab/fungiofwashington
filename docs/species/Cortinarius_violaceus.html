Cortinarius violaceus

Date: October 13, 2025

1. Taxonomy & Nomenclature

  • Scientific Name (current): Cortinarius violaceus

  • Synonyms / Former Names: Agaricus violaceus L., Cortinarius violaceus (L.) Fr., Inoloma violaceum (L. ex Fr.) Wunsche

  • Common Name(s): Violet cort, Violet webcap, Cap Gweog Fioled (Welsh)

  • Taxonomic Authority / Citation: (L.) Gray

  • Higher Classification (Kingdom / Phylum / Class / Order / Family): Fungi / Basidiomycota / Agaricomycetes / Agaricales / Cortinariaceae

2. Morphology & Identification

  • Fruiting Body Description: The fruiting body takes the shape of a gilled mushroom that can be 4-10 cm wide and 6-15 cm tall. It has a violet cap and stipe and dark violet, blackish gills. It is generally textured with small hairs and scales.

  • Cap / Pileus Characteristics: The cap is typically 4-10 cm across and is somewhat velvety, roughened by many hairs and small scales. It tends to crack when dry and is colored a deep blackish violet to deep purple. Unlike many other Cortinarius species, the cap is not slimy.

  • Gills / Pores / Spines / Teeth / Etc.: The gills have an adnate attachment to the stem. They are covered by a cobwebby purple partial veil when young, but this disappears quickly. They begin as dark purple, become grayish to blackish, and end as rusty brown.

  • Stipe / Stem / Attachment / Ring / Volva / Base: The stipe is 6-15 cm long, with a base ranging from bulbous to slightly thickened, and has a partial veil. It is somewhat hairy and is not slimy. Over time, the stem darkens deeply, indicating age.

  • Spore Print Color: Rusty brown or cinnamon.

  • Microscopic Features: Spores are 11.5-14.5 x 7-9 µm and are ellipsoid to amygadaliform (almond-shaped), with a moderately verrucose (warty) surface texture. The cheilocystidia (edge of gills) and pleurocystidia (face of gills) are fusoid-ventricose (spindle-shaped, wider in the middle), with a long neck. It is the only Cortinarius species with clearly defined cystidia on its gills.

  • Other Diagnostic Characters: It is described by various sources as having a cedarwood smell, a sweet fragrance, or no distinct smell. It turns red when it comes into contact with potassium hydroxide (KOH).

  • Similar Species / Potential Confusions:

    • Viscid violet cort (Cortinarius iodes)

    • Gassy webcap (Cortinarius traganus)

    • Silvery-violet cort (Cortinarius alboviolaceus)

    • Amethyst deceiver (Laccaria amethystina)

    • Blewit (Clitocybe nuda)

3. Distribution & Habitat in Washington

  • Range (counties or regions): Temperate areas in the Northern Hemisphere (found in both North America and Europe).

  • Geographic Distribution in WA: Largely found to the west of the Cascade Mountains (in old-growth forests both inland and on the coast). Also sometimes found in the northeastern corner of the state (Pend Oreille County).

  • Elevation Range: There is no definitive elevation range for the fungus itself. However, the old-growth forests it favors occur from sea level to around 4,000 feet.

  • Habitat Types / Substrate Preferences: In Washington state, it is most often found growing in old-growth conifer forests, but it also grows in mixed woods. It grows on the ground and is often found near rotting logs.

  • Host Associations: It is more often reported with conifers in North America and deciduous forests of broadleaf trees in Europe.

4. Seasonality & Phenology

  • Months of Fruiting in WA: June through December.

  • Seasonal Notes: Peaks in October and prefers moist soil.

5. Ecology & Role

  • Ecological Function: Forms ectomycorrhizal relationships with hardwoods or conifers.

  • Substrate / Decomposition Role: It is not known as a saprotrophic mushroom.

  • Interactions: As an ectomycorrhizal fungus, it enhances nutrient and water uptake for host trees while receiving carbohydrates in return.

  • Environmental Sensitivity / Indicators: Indicates healthy old-growth forests.

6. Conservation, Abundance & Threats

  • Abundance / Rarity in WA: Commonly encountered in WA, but in small numbers.

  • Conservation Status (state, federal, red list): Not of concern.

  • Threats / Pressures: Currently, there are no specific threats to this species. However, it is assumed that when old-growth forests are impacted, this species will be as well.

  • Management or Monitoring Notes: Requires conservation of mature forest stands, avoidance of clearcutting and soil compaction, and maintenance of leaf litter, coarse woody debris (CWD), and natural hydrology.

7. Use / Ethnobiology / Toxicology

  • Edibility / Toxicity: They are considered edible, but not choice, and so are not recommended for consumption. They may taste bitter when cooked.

  • Traditional / Cultural Uses: There are currently no documented uses for this species for culinary or medicinal uses.

  • Medicinal / Bioactive Compounds: It likely contains some compounds with anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties, but more research is needed.

  • Cautions / Warnings: There are no serious warnings for consumption or use of this species. However, it has some lookalikes within the Cortinarius family that are toxic.

10. Other Notes & Observations

  • Field Notes / Observations:

    • I (Kavya) have always found this fungus growing either by itself or in small groups of 3-5.

  • Questions / Uncertainties:

    • The description of the smell is somewhat different between sources (cedar wood, vague sweetness, or no distinctive smell).

  • Fun facts

    • Its fruiting body contains around 100 times more iron than most other fungal species.
