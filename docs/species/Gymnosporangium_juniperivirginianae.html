Gymnosporangium juniperi-virginianae

Date: October 13, 2025

1. Taxonomy & Nomenclature

  • Scientific Name (current): Gymnosporangium juniperi-virginianae
  • Synonyms / Former Names: Obligate Synonyms: Podisoma juniperi-virginianae; Tremella juniperi-virginianae; Taxonomic Synonyms: Gymnosporangium virginianum; Aecidium juniperi-virginianae; Gymnosporangium/Podisoma macropus; Roestelia/Aecidium/Caeoma pyrata/pyratum/piratum
  • Common Names: Cedar-apple Rust; Juniper-apple Rust; Cedar Rust
  • Taxonomic Authority / Citation: Gymnosporangium juniperi-virginianae Schweinitz, Schriften Naturf. Ges. Leipzig (1822)
  • Higher Classification (Kingdom / Phylum / Class / Order / Family): Kingdom Fungi / Phylum Basidiomycota / Subphylum Pucciniomycotina / Class Pucciniomycetes / Order Rust Fungi / Suborder Uredinineae / Family Gymnosporangiaceae / Genus Cedar Rusts (Gymnosporangium)

2. Morphology & Identification

  • Fruiting Body Description:
    Telial Stage: 6-50mm reddish-brown round galls hydrate to produce 10-20mm long bright orange gelatinous telia horns that produce teliospores, that when germinated, produce basidia that release basidiospores to be carried to apple leaves by the wind.

    Aecial Stage: yellow-orange to orange-red pycnia flask-like lesions on upper apple leaf surfaces that produce pycniospores and a sticky substance to attract insects which transport pycniospores between lesions. Tube-like orange-yellow aecia form on the on undersurface of leaves, and produce aeciospores to be carried to cedar trees by the wind.

  • Cap / Pileus Characteristics: N/A

  • Gills / Pores / Spines / Teeth / Etc.: Basidia formed at skin of telia horns

  • Stipe / Stem / Attachment / Ring / Volva / Base: Galls begin as small bumps on the woody portion of the plant such as needle bases or through cracks of branches.

  • Spore Print Color: White/cream spores, unobtainable print

  • Microscopic Features: two-celled teliospores; basidiospores, pycniospores, aeciospores

  • Other Diagnostic Characters: Telial Stage: cycles between orange gelatinous cones (wet) and dark brown threads (dry) 8-10 times during the spring

  • Similar Species / Potential Confusions: Gymnotelium blasdaleanum and Puccinia coronata; other Gymnosporangiums: G. clavariiforme, G. clavipes, G. corniculans, G. cornutum

3. Distribution & Habitat in Washington

  • Range (counties or regions): North America, including Central Eastern US & Canada, anywhere with overlap of Junipers and Crab/Apple trees
  • Geographic Distribution in WA: mostly Puget Sound Region and Islands; borders near with Idaho
  • Elevation Range: 150-1500m
  • Habitat Types / Substrate Preferences: Apple tree leaves and Juniper leaves and branches
  • Host Associations: Telial Host: Eastern Red Cedar (Juniperus virginiana) (and other Juniper spp.); Aecial Host: Rosaceous family commonly including apples and crab apples (Malus), as well as hawthorn (Crataegus) or serviceberry (Amelanchier)

4. Seasonality & Phenology

  • Months of Fruiting in WA: April-June
  • Seasonal Notes: Telial fruiting in spring with rainfall and stops when dry and warm. Aecial development throughout Summer and Autumn, pycniospores go dormant for winter up to 2 years, gall formation slowly develops until spring.

5. Ecology & Role

  • Ecological Function: non-lethal plant pathogen, reduces host fruiting quality and yield
  • Substrate / Decomposition Role: Apple tree leaves and Juniper leaves and branches
  • Interactions: Host specific to Junipers (Juniperus) and Roses (Rosaceae); sporulation assisted by insects and wind
  • Environmental Sensitivity / Indicators: N/A

6. Conservation, Abundance & Threats

  • Abundance / Rarity in WA: Uncommon, compared to East Coast region
  • Conservation Status (state, federal, red list): N/A, not assessed
  • Threats / Pressures: Forest fires, deforestation
  • Management or Monitoring Notes: Preventive fungicides and select pruning; apple orchard transportation regulations

7. Use / Ethnobiology / Toxicology

  • Edibility / Toxicity: Not edible, undesirable; unstudied
  • Traditional / Cultural Uses: Not useful; indigenous practices of controlled burns to regulate spread
  • Medicinal / Bioactive Compounds: phenolic acids (anthocyanins, purpurogallin, flavonoids, tannins, steroids, tritepenes, alkaloids, lipophillic components
  • Cautions / Warnings: None

10. Other Notes & Observations

  • Questions / Uncertainties: Not extensively studied
  • Fun facts: Rust life cycles are cool! Also it only has four of the five rust spore types. Gymno=naked, sporangium=fruit body, hence the orange slick bodies! Different spp of the same genus develop various types of orange “horns,” such as club-horns, cones, and finger-like appendages.
